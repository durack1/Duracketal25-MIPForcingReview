\begin{abstract}
Climate models are our most powerful tools for investigating global and regional climate changes caused by human and natural external forcing agents. These models are based on mathematical representations of the physics, chemistry, and biology of the climate system. Standardised experiments are constructed to quantify the climate model responses to externally imposed conditions, with these conditions often representing the observed past, or multiple hypothesized futures. These external imposed “forcing” conditions are captured in unique climate relevant agents, such as the natural influence of stratospheric sulphate aerosols from a large volcanic explosion, or human-driven components, such as enhancement to atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations as a response to fossil fuel burning, or historical (or future) land use changes across the globe in response to changing human development.

The input4MIPs (input datasets for Model Intercomparison Projects) project was initiated to collect the forcing datasets produced for the 6th Phase of the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project (CMIP6; \citet{eyring_overview_2016}) available through the Earth System Grid Federation (ESGF; \citet{williams_global_2016}). The various datasets were collected to externally force climate models for the pre-industrial control (piControl), AMIP (Atmospheric Model Intercomparison Project), and historical experiments. Additional datasets are needed for many of the experiments that were performed as part of the CMIP6-endorsed community model intercomparison projects (MIPs; \citet{eyring_overview_2016}). Earlier versions of many of these datasets were used in the previous CMIP5 Project \citep{taylor_overview_2012} and before that in CMIP3 \citep{meehl_global_2007}, with far simpler configurations used in the preceding phases CMIP2 \citep{meehl_global_1995}), CMIP1 \citep{meehl_intercomparison_1997} and both AMIP2 and AMIP1 \citep{gleckler_amip_1999,gates_amip_1991}.

This paper describes the evolution of the CMIP6 project beyond that defined in \citet{eyring_overview_2016}. The project continued to grow as the phase was finalized, expanding from around 190 to 322 experiments at completion, with these experiments shared across a total of 23 community MIPs (Durack et al., 2025 [in prep]).

Climate “forcings” are natural and anthropogenic (human-sourced) agents that influence the sign and magnitude of global and regional climate variability and change. For the CMIP6 historical experiment, the natural forcing agents include the observed incoming shortwave/solar radiation from the sun and the natural emission of sulphate aerosols from volcanic eruptions that have occurred throughout Earth’s history. For the CMIP6 historical experiment, anthropogenic forcing agents included the increasing atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations (and other greenhouse gas species) that have been observed primarily due to fossil fuel burning, land use changes associated with human development, ozone, and nitrogen, short-lived climate forcers (SLCFs). \section{What is climate forcing?}
Climate change results from natural and human modulation of atmospheric constituents (aerosols, volcanic emissions, greenhouse gases, etc), land use, and changes to other Earth system components. These natural or anthropogenic climate drivers are termed “climate forcing” agents. Some forcing agents are themselves responses or feedbacks to observed changes underway, like terrestrial freshwater from the Greenland/Antarctic Ice Sheet or glacial melt added to the ocean as the Earth warms, amongst other “forced” changes.

For most of the Anthropocene, Earth maintained a stable energy budget, with incoming and outgoing energy in balance. However, an imbalance began to emerge through human activities in the 20th century, driven by changes to climate forcing. The climate "forcing" agents can be identified by their sources, either "natural" due to influences of which humans have no direct influence or "anthropogenic" due to human activities. In addition, feedbacks in the Earth system can magnify the radiative impact of these forcing agents, either with positive (warming) or negative (dampening) impacts on Earth's observed global mean temperature, amongst numerous other Earth climate system markers.

The source of these agents and their climate roles differ. In addition, where the agents get to in the atmosphere (or more broad climate system) can strongly affect their impact. For example, sulfate aerosols from volcanic eruptions have a strong climate effect, reflecting incoming solar radiation, and cooling the planet. But if these sulfate aerosols get into the stratosphere, they have a far more substantial and sustained climate impact. The impact and roles of forcing get more complicated as the simulated system becomes more realistic - not only the what but where the forcing agent is found in the system begins to matter.


\section{Simulating Earth’s climate}

The evolution of climate models as tools to simulate and better understand our climate extends back to the mid-20th century. General Circulation Models (GCMs), which were crude simulations of the atmosphere only, were first used to attempt to model the evolution of weather as short-term forecasting tools. Simulations of this kind were collated in the Atmospheric Model Intercomparison Project (AMIP). Over the subsequent decades, model complexity continued to advance, with the first "coupled" models developed in the late 1960s incorporating an active ocean, sea ice, and land, coupled to the atmosphere.

The evolution continued in the 1990s, and by this stage, model complexity had improved to realize the first "historical" simulations, where time-varying climate forcing could be used as inputs to drive a model simulation to approximate our observed past. In addition, a broad international community had started collaborating to develop standardized experimental protocols, allowing individual model simulations to be collated and compared. The CMIP project collected these simulations, and this activity extends to the present day. As these activities matured, the experimental protocols evolved in their realism, from idealized to “historical” simulations that began to approximate Earth’s observed climate record. The third phase, CMIP3 and the "20th Century Climate in Coupled Models" 20C3M experiment was the first time a comprehensive set of observed climate forcing agents had been used as coupled model simulation inputs. Forcing needs and their complexity have evolved dramatically from this initial step, with atmospheric chemistry, active carbon cycling, and biogeochemistry augmenting what models are keeping track of and what they need to externally "force" them to reproduce Earth’s observed history.

For early AMIP phases, models used fixed or climatological external forcing data, targeting present-day (then 1990) control simulations or simplistic idealized transient experiments (Durack et al., 2025). For CMIP3, modeling groups began collating external forcing datasets to drive their historical simulations, including the start of numerous time-varying greenhouse gas concentrations, ozone and sulfate aerosols, including those from volcanic eruptions, solar irradiance, land use changes, and biomass burning. As model complexity increased in CMIP5, additional aerosol and short-lived climate forced (SLCF) species were also generated, and this trend continues into CMIP6 with more chemical species, and anthropogenic gridded emissions also become available to drive climate models with interactive carbon cycles.

In CMIP3 and CMIP5, the lack of forcing coordination across modeling groups created more community work, with modeling groups having to meet their own forcing needs, introducing inconsistencies across historical model simulations. For CMIP6, a coordinated approach to collecting forcing datasets was established, facilitating the interaction of the forcing dataset providers and the modeling groups using these data, and reducing the duplicated individual efforts. The Earth System Grid Federation (ESGF) input datasets for Model Intercomparison Projects (input4MIPs) activity was established, collating forcing data serving CMIP6 needs, and 15 of the CMIP6 endorsed MIPs that comprised the phase (Durack et al., 2018). This led to numerous improvements and enabled a project expansion that delivered more than 15 petabytes of the latest climate model datasets to a broad and growing international community (Durack et al., 2025).
